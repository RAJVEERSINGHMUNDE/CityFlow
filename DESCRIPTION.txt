CityFlow is a Graph-AI and Operations-Research digital twin that helps
traffic-operations teams in Bengaluru prepare for, respond to, and learn
from every kind of event-driven congestion — from a 35,000-attendee
cricket match at M. Chinnaswamy Stadium, to a multi-axle breakdown
stranding traffic on the Outer Ring Road, to a tree-fall blocking a
residential junction, to a sudden water-logging during monsoon. It is
built specifically for Problem Statement 2 of the hackathon — "Event-
Driven Congestion (Planned & Unplanned)" — and directly tackles the
three pain points the brief calls out: event impact is not quantified
in advance, resource deployment is experience-driven, and there is no
post-event learning system.

CityFlow replaces all three with a single, end-to-end data-driven
pipeline that takes an event record (lat/lon, cause, time, expected
attendance, corridor, vehicle type, free-text description) and returns
an actionable operations plan in under fifteen seconds. That plan
includes a predicted severity level (Green / Amber / Red), a predicted
clearance time with worst-case band, a pre-event traffic impact
forecast (person-delay minutes, queue length, area congestion index,
estimated vehicle count), up to three arterial diversion routes that
have been verified to avoid the closed edges and to actually save time,
a list of validated police barricade locations that do not trap
vehicles into U-turns, an officer count and shift duration grounded in
a Cox Proportional Hazards survival model, and a "What history says"
panel that pulls in the count and cause breakdown of similar incidents
within two kilometres.

The system is built on the real Bengaluru road network — 155,373 nodes
and 393,731 edges, downloaded once from OpenStreetMap and cached
locally for sub-second subgraph extraction. A reverse-Breadth-First-
Search shockwave propagates capacity decay upstream from the event
epicenter using the Bureau of Public Roads formula; once a road's
volume-to-capacity ratio crosses 0.85, a strict three-times travel-
time penalty is applied so Dijkstra's algorithm is forced to find a
genuinely free-flowing detour. Per-road-class hourly multipliers
(motorways amplify the rush-hour signal, residential and service roads
dampen it) make the recommended route actually differ between peak and
off-peak, rather than collapsing to the same shortest path at every
time of day. A Gradient Boosting regressor plus a Random Forest
classifier — trained on 8,173 historical Bengaluru incidents — predict
the severity level and the resolution time. A LaBSE multilingual
sentence encoder reads the operator's Kannada or English radio report
and produces a disruption probability that scales the edge capacity
drop on a corridor-conditioned basis, so a "heavy traffic" report on
Tumkur Road hits the network harder than the same words on a quiet
service lane. Barricade placement uses a continuous-flow algorithm
that walks upstream from each closed edge to the first node with a
safe out-degree, so vehicles are always redirected to an alternate
exit instead of being trapped at a dead end.

After the event, the operator logs the actual resolution time, the
number of officers deployed, the number of barricades used, the
observed severity, and whether the diversion was effective. Every ten
such feedback entries, a background thread re-fits the five linear
manpower weights via numpy least squares and re-trains the NLP
logistic head on the union of original and feedback embeddings, so
the system never forgets what it already knew. Forecast error,
diversion success rate, and total recorded outcomes are exposed
through a single /api/feedback/summary endpoint, giving traffic
operations a real institutional memory instead of one-shot gut calls.

The user-facing experience is a dark-themed React/Vite dashboard that
walks the operator through five steps: what is happening, how bad is
it, what does the city look like during this (a side-by-side map
showing the do-nothing baseline and the CityFlow plan), what does
CityFlow recommend, and after the event, log what really happened.
Three pre-loaded demo events — the cricket match, a heavy breakdown,
and a Kannada radio report — let a reviewer see the full range of
capability in under a minute. A built-in glossary, FAQ, and about
panel explain every metric in plain language. The backend is a Flask
API on port 8000 with a SQLite operational memory; the frontend is
served by Vite on port 3000 and proxies /api and /maps to the
backend, with a Cloudflare Pages Functions reverse proxy already
wired up for production deployment. The full project is open source,
runs end-to-end on a laptop, and is ready to be fronted by any live
traffic feed (HERE, TomTom, or a control-room CCTV pipeline) in place
of the current historical-replay adapter.
